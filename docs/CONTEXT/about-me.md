# About Me

## Role
I'm a content designer at a Canadian university. My work sits at the intersection of web governance and communication — I'm responsible for the quality, accuracy, and proper management of content across many university websites, which collectively span thousands of web pages.

## What I Actually Do
My day-to-day work falls into four main areas:
- **Content auditing** — reviewing web pages for problems like outdated information, accessibility issues, poor structure, or content that doesn't meet standards
- **Writing and editing** — drafting or rewriting web copy so it meets our standards and serves users well
- **Documentation and reporting** — writing guides, standards documents, audit summaries, and status updates for internal and external audiences
- **Stakeholder communication** — emailing faculty, staff, and department contacts to request content updates, explain web standards, follow up on issues, or navigate pushback


## My Team
I'm part of a team of about 10 people who are being centralized into one web content unit. Most of my teammates were previously the dedicated web person for a specific faculty — they still report to their faculty but are slowly being brought under a unified central team. I'm one of the few without a faculty affiliation, which means I tend to play a broader UX and content strategy role, trying to guide the overall direction rather than serving a single faculty.

This creates a particular kind of challenge: I often need to influence both my teammates (who have their own faculty loyalties and ways of doing things) and external stakeholders, without having formal authority over either. Persuasion, trust-building, and good communication are the main levers I have.

## The Nature of My Work
A big part of what I do is managing change through other people. I don't control most of the content directly — I rely on stakeholders across the university to act on my recommendations. That means a lot of my skill goes into communication: being clear, being persuasive without being confrontational, and knowing when to push and when to give people an easy on-ramp.

The scale is large. Thousands of pages, many sites, many stakeholders. A common scenario is managing large audit or cleanup projects — coordinating many different stakeholders through a structured process over time. Prioritization and staying organized matter a lot.

## Context to Keep in Mind
- I build many tools to help myself and my team, and these often take the form of Chrome extensions that analyze and then output csvs... or they take action on my behalf. I use extensions because a lot of my work happens on the backend of a Drupal system that you don't have access to. 
- My stakeholders include faculty, department administrators, web editors, and senior staff — a wide range of technical comfort levels and motivations
- Universities have a lot of institutional inertia. Change is slow and politics matter
- Accessibility and plain language are core parts of my standards work... I'm more interested in being clear than sounding complicated and intelligent
- I often know what needs to happen but need to find the right way to say it
