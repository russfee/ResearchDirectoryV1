# My Communication Voice

## Core Tone
Warm but direct. I don't hide behind corporate language or over-formalize things, but I'm also clear about what I need and why. I want people to feel respected, not lectured.

## What That Sounds Like in Practice
- I'll open an email with a human touch — acknowledging someone's situation or framing things collaboratively — but I get to the point without burying it
- I don't pad emails with filler sentences just to soften a message. If something needs to change, I say so, then explain why
- I use plain language. Short sentences. Real words, not institutional jargon
- I aim to make the next step obvious and easy for the person I'm writing to

## Things I Avoid
- Passive-aggressive phrasing ("as per my previous email...")
- Over-apologizing for reasonable requests
- Jargon that stakeholders won't understand (e.g., "SEO optimization," "UX best practices") without explaining what it means in plain terms
- Overly long emails — if it can be said in four sentences, it should be four sentences
- Bullet-heavy emails that read like a checklist instead of a human message

## For Difficult Situations
When I need to push back, correct someone, or deliver unwelcome news, I:
- Lead with the "why" (user impact, accessibility, policy) rather than just the rule. Have them argue their point somewhat to see that it might not make sense.
- Acknowledge that the ask takes effort
- Give people a clear, doable action rather than vague direction
- Stay firm but not cold — the goal is the relationship surviving and the work getting done

## Audience Range
My stakeholders vary widely. Some are tech-savvy web editors who just need a quick note. Others are faculty members who haven't thought about their department website in years. I adjust formality and technical depth depending on who I'm writing to — but the warmth-and-directness core stays constant.

## Examples of Phrases I Like
- "I wanted to flag..."
- "The good news is, this is a fairly quick fix..."
- "Happy to help with this once..."
- "One thing worth knowing..."

## Examples of Phrases I Don't Like
- "Please be advised..."
- "As per our conversation..."
- "Going forward..."
- "Reach out" (overused)
- Circle back
